Microcontroller Lab: The Arduino UNO Architecture

Back to Lab

The Arduino UNO is an open-source microcontroller board based on the ATmega328P chip. Think of it as a tiny, self-contained computer that can read inputs (like light on a sensor or a finger on a button) and turn them into outputs (like activating a motor or turning on an LED).

The Hardware Breakdown

1. Digital I/O Pins (Pins 0-13)

  • These pins can only read or output two states: HIGH (5V) or LOW (0V).
  • Perfect for turning LEDs on/off, or reading standard push buttons.
  • Pins marked with a tilde (~) can simulate analog outputs using Pulse Width Modulation (PWM).

2. Analog Input Pins (Pins A0-A5)

  • Unlike digital pins, these can read a continuous range of voltages from 0V to 5V.
  • The ATmega328P has a 10-bit Analog-to-Digital Converter (ADC), meaning it translates that 0-5V range into a readable integer between 0 and 1023.
  • Essential for variable sensors like potentiometers, temperature sensors, or photoresistors.

3. The Power Section

  • 5V & 3.3V Pins: Regulated power outputs to supply your breadboard circuits.
  • GND: Ground pins to complete your circuits.
  • Vin: Used to power the Arduino using an external battery (usually 7V-12V) instead of the USB cable.
Hardware Warning: The Arduino UNO's internal processor operates at 5 Volts. If you apply a voltage higher than 5V to any of the Digital or Analog pins, you will permanently fry the ATmega328P chip!

Advanced Architecture Facts

4. The Heartbeat: 16 MHz Quartz Crystal

  • If you look closely at the board, you will see a small silver oval component. This is the 16 MHz oscillator.
  • It acts as the clock, providing the rhythmic pulse that dictates the speed at which the microcontroller executes instructions.
  • This means your ATmega328P chip can process roughly 16 million instructions per second!

5. Memory Breakdown (Where your data lives)

  • Flash Memory (32 KB): This is where your compiled C sketch is stored. It is non-volatile, meaning your code stays safely saved even when the battery is unplugged.
  • SRAM (2 KB): This is where your temporary variables live while the code is running. Because it is so small, creating massive arrays of data will cause your Arduino to quietly crash!
  • EEPROM (1 KB): A tiny space used to save long-term data (like calibration settings or state history) that needs to survive power cycles.

6. Serial Communication Protocols

The Arduino relies on standard engineering protocols to talk to other microchips and computers:

  • UART (Pins 0 & 1): Universal Asynchronous Receiver-Transmitter. This is how the board talks to your laptop via USB to print text to the Serial Monitor.
  • I2C (Pins A4 & A5): The "Inter-Integrated Circuit" protocol. It allows you to connect dozens of sensors (like OLED screens) using only two wires (SDA and SCL).
  • SPI (Pins 10, 11, 12, 13): Serial Peripheral Interface. A very high-speed connection used for SD card readers and Wi-Fi modules.
