Beginner's Lab: Classification of Wires

Back to Lab

Not all wires are created equal. Choosing the wrong type of wire for a breadboard can cause loose connections, frustrating shorts, or even broken pins inside your board. Let's break down the cables you will actually use.

1. Core Types: Solid vs. Stranded

Solid Core Wire (The Breadboard Champion)

  • Made of a single, thick piece of copper wire.
  • Pros: Holds its shape when bent, making it perfect for keeping breadboards neat and flat. Pushes easily into breadboard holes.
  • Cons: Brittle. If you bend it back and forth too many times, it will snap.

Stranded Wire (The Flexible Connector)

  • Made of many tiny, hair-like copper strands bundled together.
  • Pros: Extremely flexible. Great for wires that need to move, like connecting a breadboard to a robotic arm or moving chassis.
  • Cons: A nightmare for breadboards. The tiny strands will fray and mush when you try to push them into a hole, causing short circuits.

2. Pre-Made Jumper Wires (Dupont Cables)

These are the colorful wires with plastic connectors on the ends. They come in three mandatory configurations for lab work:

Male-to-Male (M-M)

  • Has metal pins sticking out of both ends.
  • Use Case: Connecting two different points on your breadboard together, or connecting a breadboard to an Arduino UNO header.

Male-to-Female (M-F)

  • Has one pin and one empty socket.
  • Use Case: Connecting a sensor module (which usually has pins sticking out) directly into your breadboard.

Female-to-Female (F-F)

  • Has empty sockets on both ends.
  • Use Case: Connecting two sensor modules together, or extending the length of male pins.
SpecialNote on Gauge (AWG): Wire thickness is measured in American Wire Gauge (AWG). The *higher* the number, the *thinner* the wire. For breadboards, standard **22 AWG solid core wire** is the absolute perfect fit. Anything thicker (like 18 AWG) will permanently stretch and ruin your breadboard's internal spring clips!
